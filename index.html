An introduction to the Matchbook Morning Meeting Playbook

The Matchbook Way

At Matchbook Learning, we believe that culture is practiced before pressure. Traditional, unstructured check-ins often spiral into lost instructional minutes. We are moving away from that reality.

The Morning Meeting is our daily Tier 1 culture anchor. It is a highly strategic, 15-minute routine designed to engineer a productive, regulated classroom environment from the minute the bell rings.

We ask students to be Safe, Respectful, and Responsible all day long. The Morning Meeting is where we explicitly teach them how.

The sequence is the intervention.

Every day, we move through six predictable steps: Regulate, Orient, Connect, Practice, Commit, and Transition. This 15-minute operational shield reclaims hours of preventable disruption later in the day.

The detailed, day-by-day playbook for explicitly teaching the Morning Meeting routine during the first two weeks of school. Culture is practiced before pressure.

The Time Shift

At mastery, Morning Meeting is a crisp 15-minute routine. However, you cannot teach a new system in 15 minutes.

  • Days 1–5 (40-50 min): Explicitly teaching the "why," modelling the moves, and practice do-overs.
  • Days 6–8 (25-30 min): Students take on roles and refine transitions.
  • Days 9–10 (15-20 min): The system carries the routine. Evaluating readiness.

All-Day Integration

Morning Meeting isn't a silo. It is the anchor for the entire day. Tie academic blocks back to the morning:

  • "At Matchbook, we correct the routine, not the child. Let's reset this transition just like we practiced this morning."
  • "The Matchbook way is Safe, Respectful, and Responsible. Which one do we need to focus on right now?"

Adjustments for lower elementary, upper elementary, and middle school to ensure the routine is developmentally appropriate.

K-2
  • Use shorter regulation techniques (e.g., belly breathing).
  • Provide visual cues for each move.
  • Choose simpler share prompts ("What is your favourite colour?") before moving to deeper questions.
  • Incorporate movement during Practice (e.g., role‑play with stuffed animals).
  • Model commitments verbally and allow drawing instead of writing.
3-5
  • Increase length of share prompts.
  • Encourage students to create their own practice moves.
  • Integrate simple academic connections (e.g., recall skills).
  • Have students write commitments on sticky notes.
  • Introduce basic data entry (e.g., recording nominations).
6-8
  • Use complex share prompts that connect to identity and community.
  • Allow students to lead entire moves.
  • Integrate digital tools for commitments (e.g., Google Forms).
  • Encourage peer feedback during practice.
  • Explore restorative concepts in greater depth.

Healing-Centered Guardrail

Respecting Student Silence: Silence is a valid coping strategy for trauma. Invite participation, but never force a student to speak or demand an explanation for passing. We protect psychological safety first. The sequence itself is the intervention, even if a student is only observing.

Clarify the difference between minor routine mistakes and relational harm requiring restorative dialogue to avoid conflating compliance with accountability.

Type Examples Adult Response Repair Approach
Routine Error Forgetting to raise a hand, talking out of turn, missing a practice cue. Use a non-verbal cue or quiet reminder; reset the routine. Quick reset. "My bad." No apology required.
Relational Harm Teasing, excluding someone, disrespectful language, damaging belongings. Stop behaviour; regulate parties; schedule restorative conversation. Use Restorative Repair Framework; involve affected parties.

The Nomination Protocol

Recognition is evidence, not popularity.

To be eligible for Friday Cypher, a student must meet objective criteria tracked in the Morning Meeting app/tracker:

  • Participation: Participated at least 4 days this week.
  • Practice Fidelity: Average practice score ≥ 2 (Partial or Full).
  • Recorded Evidence: Teacher has logged at least one specific, observable action tied to S/R/R.

A structured system for making calls to families that emphasises partnership, growth, and dignity. The classroom-home channel must be bidirectional.

Purpose: To celebrate specific evidence collected during Morning Meeting.

Purpose: To identify a specific skill to practice, framing behavior as an opportunity.

A teacher-facing response system (R³ Integration) that reduces improvisation in high-stress classroom moments. The adult response is the intervention.

The Warm Demander Stance

Culturally responsive classroom management balances high personal warmth with high expectations.

  • Proximity Control (Proxemics): Move near a disruptive student calmly. Do not stand over them or violate personal space, which triggers amygdala hijack.
  • Non-Verbal Cues (Kinesics): Use a neutral facial expression. Avoid pointing fingers or aggressive postures.

Engagement & Walk-Away

When a student is in a "freeze" state or passively disengaged, do not force a public compliance test.

"Quietly place a sticky note on the student's desk with a simple instruction ('Start when you can'). Immediately turn and walk away without waiting for them to look up or speak. This provides psychological safety and gives the student's freeze response time to thaw."

Private Check-In Protocol (R³)

When a student uses the break/reset spot, the intervention is not over once they return. A private, caring check-in using the Regulate-Relate-Reason framework validates their experience and restores the relationship.

1. Regulate
Calm the nervous system before talking.

"Let's take a breath together. I'm right here with you."
2. Relate
Empathize and validate their feelings.

"I see you're frustrated. It makes sense that you felt that way when that happened."
3. Reason
Collaboratively decide how to repair.

"What do you need right now? How do we make this right?"
